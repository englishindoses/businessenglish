💡 Grammar Tip

Modal Verbs for Politeness

Modal verbs like could, would, and might soften requests and make them sound more professional.

✗ Informal / Too Direct → ✓ Polite

  • ✗ Send me the report. → ✓ Could you send me the report?
  • ✗ I want a meeting tomorrow. → ✓ Would it be possible to meet tomorrow?
  • ✗ Tell me your decision. → ✓ I was wondering if you could let me know your decision.
  • ✗ Reply by Friday. → ✓ I would appreciate it if you could reply by Friday.
  • ✗ Change the deadline. → ✓ Would you mind changing the deadline?

Key Patterns

  • Could you...? – Simple polite request
  • Would it be possible to...? – More formal
  • I was wondering if you could... – Very polite, good for sensitive requests
  • I would appreciate it if you could... – Polite with a deadline
  • Would you mind + -ing? – Polite for small favours (NOT: Would you mind to...)

Activity 1/4

Openings & Closings by Formality

The phrases you use to start and end an email set the tone. Sort these expressions by their level of formality.

Dear Mr/Ms [Surname],
I hope this email finds you well.
Kind regards,
Yours sincerely,
Dear [First name],
I hope you're doing well.
Best regards,
Many thanks,
Hi [Name],
Hope you had a good weekend!
Thanks!
Cheers,

👔 Formal

First contact, senior people, external clients

🤝 Neutral

Regular contacts, colleagues in other teams

😊 Informal

Close colleagues, people you email often

💡 Tip: Mirror the Other Person

A good rule is to match the formality of the person you're replying to. If they use "Hi" and your first name, you can do the same. If they start with "Dear Mr...", stay formal until they become more casual.

Activity 3/4

Common Email Phrasal Verbs

These phrasal verbs appear frequently in professional emails. Click each card to see the meaning and an example.

follow up (on)

= contact again about something
"I'm just following up on my previous email."

get back to

= reply to someone later
"I'll get back to you by Friday."

look into

= investigate or examine
"I'll look into this and let you know."

set up

= arrange or organise
"Could we set up a call for next week?"

put together

= create or compile
"I've put together a summary for you."

fill (someone) in

= give someone information they missed
"Can you fill me in on what was decided?"

keep (someone) posted

= continue to give updates
"I'll keep you posted on any changes."

run (something) by

= check with someone before deciding
"I wanted to run this idea by you first."

Activity 4/4

Email vs Chat: Different Phrases

The same message often needs different language depending on whether you're writing an email or a chat message. Complete each pair with the correct phrase.

Giving a quick update:

📧 Email: " the meeting has been moved to 3pm."

💬 Chat: " – meeting moved to 3pm"

Asking for information:

📧 Email: " send me the latest sales figures."

💬 Chat: " – do you have the latest sales figures?"

Saying you'll reply later:

📧 Email: " with more details by end of day."

💬 Chat: " 👍"

Thanking someone:

📧 Email: " – I really appreciate it."

💬 Chat: " 🙏"

💡 More Useful Chat Phrases

FYI – for your information (just so you know)
Let me know! – asking for a response
On it! – I'm working on this now
Makes sense – I understand / I agree
