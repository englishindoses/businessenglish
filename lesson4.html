Activity 2

Starting & Ending Emails

The way you start and end an email sets the tone. Match these openings and closings to the appropriate level of formality.

Email Openings

Sort these greetings and opening lines from most formal to least formal.

Dear Mr/Ms [Surname],
I hope this email finds you well.
Dear [First name],
I hope you're doing well.
Thank you for getting back to me.
Hi [Name],
Hope you're well!
Hope you had a good weekend.
Just a quick one...

👔 Formal

First contact, senior people, clients you don't know well

🤝 Neutral

Regular clients, colleagues in other departments

😊 Informal

Close colleagues, people you email often

💡 Tip: Mirror the Other Person

A good rule is to match the formality of the person you're replying to. If they use "Hi" and your first name, you can do the same. If they use "Dear Mr...", stay formal until they become more casual.

Activity 3

Structuring Your Email: Connectors

Good emails are easy to follow. Connectors (linking words) help your reader understand how your ideas connect. Click each card to see examples.

Stating Purpose

I'm writing to... inquire about / follow up on / confirm
I wanted to... let you know / check in about / ask

Adding Information

In addition, ...
Furthermore, ...
I should also mention that...
On top of that, ...

Contrasting / However

However, ...
On the other hand, ...
That said, ...
Although X, Y...

Giving Reasons

This is because...
The reason for this is...
Due to [noun],
Since / As [clause],

Results / Consequences

As a result, ...
Therefore, ...
This means that...
Consequently, ...

Referring to Attachments

Please find attached...
I've attached...
As you can see in the attached...
The attached document shows...

💡 Connector Position

Most connectors work at the start of a sentence followed by a comma: "However, we need to reconsider." Some also work mid-sentence: "We need, however, to reconsider." The mid-sentence position sounds more formal.

Activity 4

Making Polite Requests

In professional emails, how you ask for something matters as much as what you ask for. Modal verbs (can, could, would, might) help soften requests and sound more professional.

The Politeness Scale

The more words you add, the more polite (and indirect) your request sounds:

Directness Example
Direct (can sound rude) Send me the report.
Neutral Can you send me the report?
Polite Could you send me the report?
More polite Could you possibly send me the report?
Very polite I was wondering if you could send me the report?
Most polite I was wondering if you might be able to send me the report?

💡 Key Patterns

Would/Could + base verb: "Would you check..." / "Could you confirm..."
Would you mind + -ing: "Would you mind sending..." (NOT: Would you mind to send)
I would appreciate it if you could: "I would appreciate it if you could let me know..."

🇧🇷 Brazilian Learner Note: Structure after 'would like'

✗ I would like that you send me the report.

✓ I would like you to send me the report.

In Portuguese, 'gostaria que você enviasse' uses 'que' (that). In English, 'would like' is followed directly by object + infinitive: 'would like [someone] to [do something]'.

Analysing a Model Email

Read this follow-up email and identify the language features we've studied. Discuss with your teacher.

Model Email: Following up on a proposal

Subject: Following up on our proposal

Dear Ms Carvalho,

I hope you're doing well.

I'm just following up on the proposal I sent over last week regarding the marketing campaign. I was wondering if you've had a chance to look it over?

If you have any questions or would like me to clarify anything, I'd be happy to set up a call at your convenience. Could you possibly let me know your thoughts by the end of the week?

We're keen to move forward and would appreciate your feedback.

Many thanks,
Sarah

Activity 7

Email vs Chat: Side by Side

Compare how the same information might be communicated in an email vs. a company chat message.

📧 Email Version

Hi Tom,

I hope you're well. I'm writing to let you know that the client meeting has been moved to 3pm tomorrow. Please let me know if that works for you.

Thanks,
Ana

💬 Chat Version

Hey! Quick heads up - client meeting moved to 3pm tomorrow. You ok with that?

🗣️ Key Differences

Email: Greeting + sign-off, complete sentences, "I'm writing to", polite "please"
Chat: No greeting/sign-off needed, fragments OK, casual phrases ("heads up"), direct questions

💡 Useful Chat Phrases

Quick question – (before asking something)
Heads up: (to give a quick update or warning)
FYI (for your information – just so you know)
Let me know! (asking for a response)
Thanks! or 👍 (acknowledging something)
