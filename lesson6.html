Grammar Review

Second Conditional for Diplomatic Suggestions

One useful way to make suggestions sound less direct in meetings is to use the second conditional. It frames your idea as a hypothetical possibility, which makes it feel less like a demand and more like an invitation to consider.

📘 Structure

  • If + past simple, ... would / could / might + infinitive.

📘 Examples

  • Direct: "We should postpone the launch."
    Diplomatic: "If we postponed the launch, we would have more time to prepare."
  • Direct: "You need to increase the budget."
    Diplomatic: "If we increased the budget, we could deliver better results."
  • Direct: "Let's hire more staff."
    Diplomatic: "What if we hired more staff? Would that help?"
  • Direct: "We should change suppliers."
    Diplomatic: "If we changed suppliers, we might get better prices."

💡 Remember

The second conditional is one of several tools for being diplomatic — you'll also learn hedging language, tentative opinion phrases, and concern-based disagreement in this lesson. Use whichever feels most natural for the situation.

Hedging

Softening What You Say

Hedging means making your statements less absolute or less direct. In English, it's a key part of sounding professional, especially when giving opinions, making suggestions, or delivering news that might not be well received.

How Do We Hedge in English?

  • Adverbs: perhaps, possibly, probably, apparently, seemingly.
    "This is possibly the best approach for now."
  • Phrases: somewhat, to some extent, in a way, more or less.
    "I somewhat agree with the first option."
  • Verbs: seem, appear, might, could.
    "It seems like we need more time."

❌ Without Hedging

  • "This plan won't work."
  • "You're wrong about the timeline."
  • "We need to change suppliers."

✅ With Hedging

  • "This plan might need some adjustments."
  • "The timeline could be somewhat optimistic."
  • "Perhaps we should consider looking at other suppliers."

Expertise-Based Opinions

In high-stakes meetings, anchoring your opinion in evidence or your professional role makes you more persuasive and less likely to shut down the conversation.

  • "From a [department] perspective, I'm concerned that..."
    e.g. "From a finance perspective, I'm concerned that this will exceed our quarterly budget."
  • "Based on the data we have, it's clear that..."
    e.g. "Based on the data we have, it's clear that customer retention has dropped."
  • "In my experience with [area], this approach often..."
    e.g. "In my experience with product launches, this approach often creates bottlenecks."

Disagreeing Without "But"

The word "but" often cancels everything that came before it, making the other person feel dismissed. Compare these two approaches:

❌ With "But"

  • "I agree with you, but I think we should wait."
  • "That's a good idea, but the budget won't allow it."

✅ Without "But"

  • "I agree with you. My concern is that we might not be ready yet."
  • "That's a good idea. How does that account for the current budget constraints?"

Say It Diplomatically

Below are five things someone might say in a meeting that sound too direct or rude. Read each one aloud, then give a more professional and diplomatic alternative using any of the language from today's lesson. Your teacher will give you feedback on the spot.

1"That idea is never going to work."

💬 Say this diplomatically. Think about hedging or sharing a concern.

2"You clearly haven't thought about the costs."

💬 Reframe this using an expertise-based phrase or a concern.

3"We need to cancel the project immediately."

💬 Turn this into a diplomatic suggestion. Consider using the second conditional.

💬 Disagree without using "but." Focus on sharing your perspective.

💬 Soften this statement using hedging language.

The Delayed Project

The New Office

Your company is considering opening a new office in another city. A colleague is enthusiastic about the location, but you have concerns about the costs and logistics.

Practice: Hedging, tentative opinions, agreeing partially then raising concerns.

The Marketing Strategy

A colleague has proposed a new marketing strategy that involves a large investment in social media. You partially agree with the direction but believe the budget allocation is wrong.

Practice: Partial agreement, concern-based disagreement, data-driven opinions.
